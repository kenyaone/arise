💼 What Is a Career?

A career is a long-term professional journey, not just a single job. Understanding this difference is the first step in making purposeful decisions about your future.

  • Job — short-term work done primarily for income, which may change frequently
  • Career — a purposeful long-term path made up of related roles, experiences, and growth in one field
🔑 Career A series of related occupational roles over a lifetime, often in one field, shaped by deliberate choices, education, and experience.
🇪🇦 Example — Wanjiku's Healthcare Career Wanjiku starts as a community health volunteer in Kiambu, becomes a registered nurse after training at KMTC, and eventually rises to hospital administrator at Kenyatta National Hospital. Each role is different — but all are part of one healthcare career built step by step.

🧠 Self-Assessment: Know Yourself

Before choosing a career, you must understand yourself. The VISAP model is a simple self-assessment framework used by career counsellors in Kenyan schools.

  • V — Values: What matters most to you? (helping others, earning money, creativity, leadership, faith)
  • I — Interests: What activities energise you? What do you enjoy so much you lose track of time?
  • S — Skills: What are you naturally good at — writing, maths, working with your hands, talking to people?
  • A — Abilities: What aptitudes do you have even without training — spatial reasoning, empathy, logic?
  • P — Personality: Do you prefer working alone or with people? Indoors or outdoors? Leading or supporting?
🇪🇦 Example — Otieno's VISAP Profile Otieno loves biology, spends weekends helping sick animals on his uncle's farm in Kisumu, and is known for his patience. His VISAP profile points strongly toward veterinary medicine or agricultural science — both realistic pathways after KCSE.
💡 Tip Guidance counsellors in Kenyan secondary schools are trained to help you do formal self-assessments. Book an appointment — it is free and confidential.

🏫 Understanding KUCCPS

If you plan to join a public university in Kenya, you will go through KUCCPS — the body that manages all government-sponsored university placements.

  • KUCCPS = Kenya Universities and Colleges Central Placement Service
  • Governs how KCSE graduates are placed into public universities and some colleges
  • Each degree programme has four cluster subjects — the four KCSE subjects most relevant to that course
  • Your cluster points are calculated from your grades in those four subjects and must meet the minimum cut-off for your chosen programme
  • Minimum cluster points differ by course — e.g. Medicine requires approximately 40+ points, while Education may require 20+
🔑 Cluster Points The weighted KCSE score calculated from your four cluster subjects for a specific degree programme. Different programmes have different cluster subject combinations and different minimum cut-off points.
⚠️ Important KUCCPS only places students into government-sponsored slots at public universities. Applications to private universities and TVET institutions are made separately and directly to those institutions.
💲 HELB Student Loans

The cost of university education can be a barrier for many Kenyan families. HELB exists to bridge that gap so that financial hardship does not end a student's education journey.

  • HELB = Higher Education Loans Board
  • Provides loans (money you borrow and repay after graduation) and bursaries (money given free to very needy students) to Kenyan university students
  • Applications open annually — apply early after KCSE results are released
  • Repayment begins after graduation once you are formally employed
🔑 Bursary A bursary is money given to a student that does not need to be repaid — unlike a loan. It is awarded based on financial need and is intended to support students from very poor backgrounds.
🇪🇦 Example — Kamau from Kisii Kamau scored a C+ in KCSE and gained admission to KMTC for a clinical medicine diploma. He applied for HELB funding early and was awarded a bursary that covered most of his fees. He is now in his second year, debt-free.
💡 County Bursaries Every county government in Kenya offers bursaries to local students. Visit your County Education Office or check your county government website for application details — many students miss these simply because they do not know they exist.
📋 Steps in Career Planning

Career planning is not a single decision — it is an ongoing process. Following these six steps helps you make informed, realistic choices rather than guessing or following peer pressure.

  1. Self-assess — Know your values, interests, skills, abilities, and personality (VISAP)
  2. Explore — Research careers and educational pathways that match your profile; talk to people working in those fields
  3. Decide — Shortlist 2–3 realistic options; use a pros and cons analysis to weigh each one honestly
  4. Plan — Map out the specific academic requirements, institutions, and financial steps needed to achieve your chosen career
  5. Act — Apply to schools or programmes, seek mentors in your chosen field, do job shadowing or internships where possible
  6. Review — Revisit and update your plan regularly as you gain new experience, qualifications, and insight

Scenario: Victor, 17, is told by his girlfriend: "I want us to have a baby — it will make our relationship stronger and you'll finally have a real purpose to work for. I'm ready even if you're not."
What should Victor do?
She says "You're being selfish — I thought you loved me." How does Victor respond?
✅
Chose Their Future Together — Best Outcome

Victor reframed love correctly: real care includes protecting someone's future, not just agreeing with what they say they want in the moment. In Kenya, 63% of teenage fathers are no longer present 2 years after the birth of an unplanned child. Victor's refusal protected both of them from a path with a very high failure rate.

⚠️
Protected the Future Quietly

Holding firm on abstinence while the conversation continues is the responsible choice. Victor may need to be more direct: "I love you and my answer is still no — we are not ready." That conversation is worth having.

The counsellor explains the real statistics to both of them. Victor's girlfriend is surprised by the data. She says "Maybe we should wait." What now?
✅
Counselling Changed the Trajectory

The counsellor's intervention — neutral, evidence-based — gave both of them permission to choose their futures. This is the ideal outcome: the relationship continues, the education continues, and the decision to wait is made together. This is what sexual and reproductive health education is designed to achieve.

❌
Reversing the Pressure Is Still Wrong

Victor pushing forward when she expressed doubt is the same coercion in reverse — and it leads to the same unintended pregnancy, with Victor now carrying full co-responsibility. Consent requires both people to be certain and willing, at every stage. Her doubt was the correct answer — not an opportunity.

❌
Agreement Is Not the Same as Readiness

Victor's instinct to "support her" sounds loving — but it sets aside his own assessment that he's not ready, and does not change the real-world consequences. A baby does not strengthen relationships that are not ready — it tests them severely. In Kenya, 44% of teenage mothers never return to school. 63% of teen fathers are absent within 2 years. Victor's readiness matters too.
