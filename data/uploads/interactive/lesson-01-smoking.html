🔍 Key Inquiry Question: Cigarettes are sold legally at every kiosk in Kenya — yet they kill more Kenyans each year than road accidents. How does something so deadly stay so common, and what exactly does it do inside your body?
🫁 The Lungs — First and Worst Hit

Every cigarette contains over 7,000 chemicals — 70 confirmed carcinogens. The lungs absorb all of these directly. Over time, this causes three major diseases that are all grouped under the name COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease).

🔑 Carcinogen: A cancer-causing chemical. Tobacco smoke contains tar, benzene, and formaldehyde — these directly damage lung cell DNA, causing uncontrolled cell growth (cancer).
DiseaseWhat Happens InsideSymptoms You See
Lung CancerCarcinogens mutate DNA in lung cells; tumours grow and block airwaysChronic cough, blood in sputum, chest pain, unexplained weight loss
Chronic BronchitisAirways permanently inflamed; overproduction of mucus blocks breathingDaily cough with mucus for months, wheezing, breathlessness on exertion
EmphysemaAlveoli (air sacs) permanently destroyed — gas exchange impossibleSevere breathlessness even at rest; barrel-shaped chest; blue-tinged lips
🇰🇪 Mwangi wa Kamau, Murang'a: A 47-year-old subsistence farmer who started smoking at Form 2 "to look mature around older boys." By his early 40s, he could not walk from his house to his shamba — 200 metres away — without stopping twice to catch his breath. Kenyatta National Hospital diagnosed COPD with emphysema. There is no cure. His three sons now do all the farm work while he sits on the veranda watching them. "Ukweli ni kwamba nilijisaidia kidogo sana kwa bei kubwa sana."
💡 TB Connection: Smokers are 3× more likely to contract tuberculosis — a major killer in Kenya. Tobacco smoke destroys the cilia (tiny hair-like structures) lining your airways that sweep bacteria out. Without cilia, bacteria like Mycobacterium tuberculosis reach the lungs easily.
❤️ The Heart & Blood Vessels

Every cigarette you smoke immediately raises your blood pressure and heart rate. Over years, the blood vessel walls thicken and harden — a condition called atherosclerosis — putting you at constant risk of heart attack and stroke.

🔑 Atherosclerosis: Fatty plaques build up inside arteries, narrowing them like blocked pipes. Smoking accelerates this process — it begins in teenage smokers. A clot in a narrowed artery = heart attack or stroke.
Cardiovascular ConditionRisk vs Non-SmokerThe Mechanism
Heart Attack2–4× higherPlaque blocks coronary arteries; heart muscle dies from lack of oxygen
Stroke2–3× higherBlood clot travels to brain; brain cells die within minutes
HypertensionRaised with every cigaretteNicotine constricts blood vessels; heart works harder
PAD (leg artery disease)6× higherArteries in legs blocked; tissue dies; amputation in severe cases
⚠️ Carbon Monoxide Poisoning: CO in smoke binds to red blood cells instead of oxygen — 200× more strongly. Your heart must pump 3× harder after each cigarette just to deliver adequate oxygen to your brain and muscles. This is why smokers feel "breathless" even before lung disease develops.
🇰🇪 Adhiambo, Kisumu: A 38-year-old office administrator had a stroke during a meeting at Lake Victoria offices. Colleagues thought she was having a seizure. She survived but lost movement on her left side. Her cardiologist at Jaramogi Oginga Odinga Teaching Hospital confirmed smoking was the primary cause. She was only 38. "Nilikuwa nadhani hii inaathiri wazee tu."

🇰🇪 Njeri, Nyeri: A 34-year-old primary school teacher, always mistaken for 50. Her students asked her headteacher if she was retiring soon. She also has recurring stomach ulcers that her doctor at Nyeri County Referral Hospital links directly to her 12-year smoking habit. "Sijawahi fikiria kwamba sigara inachekesha uso wangu. Nilikuwa nadhani ni stress tu."
👶 Fertility, Pregnancy & Children

Smoking harms reproduction at every stage — from conception through to a child's development years after birth.

WhoHow Smoking Harms Them
Men who smokeReduced sperm count, poor motility (swimming ability), abnormal sperm shape, erectile dysfunction from reduced blood flow
Women who smokeReduced egg quality, fallopian tube damage, earlier menopause (by 1–4 years), higher miscarriage rate
Pregnant smokersPremature birth, placenta praevia, placental abruption, ectopic pregnancy, low birth weight
Babies born to smokersSIDS (cot death) risk 3×, underdeveloped lungs, higher risk of birth defects
Children exposed to smokeAsthma, glue ear, ADHD, 40% more respiratory infections, reduced IQ development
⚠️ No Safe Level in Pregnancy: Even 1–2 cigarettes per day during pregnancy causes measurable harm to fetal development. There is no "safe" level. Carbon monoxide crosses the placenta and replaces oxygen in the fetal bloodstream.
🇰🇪 Nasieku & Lekimpu, Narok: Both smoked during her pregnancy. Their daughter was born 7 weeks premature at Narok County Hospital and spent 3 weeks in NICU. The family spent KES 80,000 on hospital bills that could have been avoided. Nasieku quit the day her daughter was born. "Kila scan, daktari alinionyesha jinsi mtoto wangu alivyopata oksijeni kidogo. Sijasahau picha hizo."

🧠 Nicotine — Why Quitting Is So Hard

People often ask: "Why don't smokers just stop?" The answer is that nicotine addiction is a physical brain disease — not a weakness of character.

🔑 How Nicotine Hijacks the Brain: Nicotine reaches the brain in 7 seconds, flooding it with dopamine — the reward chemical. Within 3–4 weeks of daily smoking, the brain reduces its own dopamine receptors and now requires nicotine just to feel normal. Withdrawal = irritability, anxiety, intense cravings, difficulty concentrating.
Why People StartThe Hidden Reality
Peer pressure at school/college90% of adult smokers started before age 18. Tobacco companies know this and market aggressively to youth.
"It relieves my stress"Nicotine only relieves the withdrawal stress IT created. Long-term, smokers have significantly higher anxiety levels than non-smokers.
"It makes me look cool/adult"This image is entirely manufactured by tobacco advertising. Tobacco companies in Kenya spend millions on it each year.
"Just trying it once"One cigarette causes measurable changes in dopamine pathways in genetically susceptible individuals.
🇰🇪 Baraka, Mombasa Old Town: Started smoking at 15 at a social gathering near Fort Jesus. "Kijana mmoja alitupa paketi na akasema angekuwa mwenzangu tu akisigara." By 18, he needed 10 cigarettes a day to function. He tried to quit 4 times before succeeding at 23 using nicotine patches from a Coast General Hospital clinic. "Haukuwa uamuzi — ulikuwa utegemezi."
💡 Quitting Works: 20 minutes after quitting, blood pressure normalises. 48 hours — CO leaves blood. 1 year — heart disease risk halved. 10 years — lung cancer risk halved. Kenya's NACADA helpline: 1192 (free, confidential).
💰 The Cost, The Law & Those Around You

Smoking harms not only the smoker but everyone around them — and Kenya's law recognises this.

💰 Financial Cost in Kenya: A cigarette at a kiosk costs KES 15–25. A pack (20) costs KES 300–500.
  • 10 cigarettes/day × KES 20 = KES 200/day
  • = KES 6,000/month — more than many school fees
  • = KES 73,000/year — a quality dairy cow, or Form 1–4 school fees

Second-hand smoke carries virtually the same 70 carcinogens as inhaled smoke. Non-smokers regularly exposed face 80–90% the same cancer risk.

  • Children with smoking parents: 40% higher asthma risk
  • Partners of smokers: 30% higher heart disease risk
  • Bar/restaurant workers before smoking bans: 20× more exposure than average smoker
🏛️ Tobacco Control Act, Kenya 2007:
  • Smoking banned in all public places, workplaces, schools, public transport
  • Selling cigarettes to anyone under 18 — criminal offence
  • Graphic health warnings mandatory on all packaging
  • Penalties: up to KES 500,000 fine or 2 years imprisonment
⚠️ Single Sticks at Kiosks: Selling single cigarettes to youths is widespread in Kenya despite being illegal. If you see this near your school, you can report it to NACADA (1192) anonymously. Three traders in Eldoret were fined KES 50,000 each in 2023 after student reports.
📝 Practice Problems
Problem 1 — Calculation: Kamau buys 2 packets of cigarettes per week at KES 400 each in Nakuru town. He has been smoking for 8 years.

(a) Calculate his total spending on cigarettes over 8 years.
(b) What could he have purchased with that money? (Give two specific Kenyan examples.)
(c) If he quit today and saved the same amount monthly, how much would he have after 2 years?

Model Answer:
(a) 2 × 400 = KES 800/week × 52 weeks = KES 41,600/year × 8 years = KES 332,800
(b) A plot of land in Nakuru outskirts / secondary school fees for all 4 years for two children
(c) KES 800/week × 52 × 2 = KES 83,200 saved
Problem 2 — Application: Wanjiru (Form 3, Murang'a) tells her friend: "My dad smokes 2 cigarettes a day — that's nothing, it can't really hurt him." Using specific evidence from this lesson, write the response Wanjiru's friend should give.

Model Answer: Even 2 cigarettes/day causes: (1) Measurable arterial wall thickening within weeks; (2) Nicotine addiction — the brain adapts; (3) Cumulative carcinogen exposure — each cigarette adds tar to airways; (4) No safe level of second-hand smoke exposure to the family; (5) KES 73,000/year financial drain at just 10/day — 2/day still costs KES 14,600/year.
Problem 3 — Analysis: "My grandfather smoked every day from age 20 and lived to 88 — so smoking clearly isn't as dangerous as people say." Evaluate this statement.

Model Answer: This is an anecdotal fallacy. One person's experience cannot override population data. Statistically, smokers die 10 years earlier on average. Individual genetic variation exists — some people are more resistant to tobacco damage — but they are exceptions. If 100 people made the same choice as the grandfather, most would suffer significantly. Scientific conclusions require thousands of cases, not one grandfather.
