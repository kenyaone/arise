Scenario: Kamau, 17, is at a friend's house after school. His friend Brian brings out what looks like a rolled cigarette and says, "Relax, it's just bhang — everyone tries it once. It's not even addictive." Brian passes it to Kamau.
What does Kamau do?
Brian laughs and says "Don't be boring — just one puff, no one will know." What does Kamau do next?
✅
Strong Refusal — Best Outcome

Kamau uses the broken record technique — repeating his refusal calmly. Brian eventually gives up. Kamau keeps his self-respect, his health, and his athletics scholarship. Key lesson: Real pressure rarely stops at the first no. Being prepared for the second ask is what separates those who stay clean from those who don't.

⚠️
Avoided — But Left the Door Open

Walking away worked this time. But Kamau didn't communicate his boundary clearly. Brian may try again next time they meet. Better approach: A clear verbal refusal + leaving. Silence can be misread as hesitation.

❌
Gave In Under Continued Pressure

This is the most dangerous moment — Kamau refused once but caved to the second ask. This is how most first-time drug use happens. Nicotine-like addiction pathways begin immediately. Brian now knows Kamau can be pushed. Next time the drug may be stronger. The refusal that matters most is the second one.

Brian responds: "One puff won't affect your training, stop being dramatic." What does Kamau do?
✅
Upgraded to Values-Based Refusal

Smart recovery. Kamau started with an excuse but moved to a stronger, values-based refusal when challenged. Key lesson: Excuses invite arguments — "training" can always be countered. "I don't want to" cannot. Excuses work sometimes, but a clear personal boundary works better.

❌
Excuses Backfired

Brian dismantled each excuse one by one until Kamau had no more. This is why excuses are a weak refusal strategy — they imply you would say yes if the excuse weren't there. A values-based refusal ("I don't use drugs, period") has no counter-argument. Practise saying no without justifying it.

⚠️
Hesitation Read as Interest

Brian interprets Kamau's silence and curiosity as wavering. He starts applying more pressure. Key lesson: In peer pressure situations, hesitation is dangerous — it signals that you can be convinced. A quick, calm, clear response is always stronger than silence. Practise: "No thanks" and look away or change the subject immediately.

❌
First Use — The Gateway Moment

"Just once" is the lie every substance tells. THC from bhang reaches the brain's reward centre in minutes, releasing dopamine. The brain notes this experience. For 1 in 10 people, regular use begins here. 90% of adults with substance use disorder say they started with "just once." Kamau's athletics scholarship, future plans, and brain health are all at risk from this moment.
