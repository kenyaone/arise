🧠 🧠 Psychological / Emotional Harm
Threats, humiliation, gaslighting, isolation, and constant criticism. These cause anxiety, depression, PTSD and low self-worth — often harder to heal than physical injuries. Emotional abuse often precedes physical violence.
❤️ ❤️ Physical Violence
Hitting, slapping, punching, kicking, burning, strangling. Causes bruising, broken bones, internal bleeding, and in severe cases death. The most visible form of GBV — but never the only form.
🟣 🟣 Sexual Violence
Any sexual act without consent — including rape, defilement, and sexual harassment. Causes physical injury, STIs, unwanted pregnancy, and severe psychological trauma. Defilement of anyone under 18 is a criminal offence in Kenya.
💰 💰 Economic Abuse
Controlling all money, preventing work or education, creating total financial dependence. Traps victims in abusive situations by removing their ability to leave safely. Very common and often invisible.
🗣️ 🗣️ Verbal Abuse
Name-calling, screaming, degrading language, constant criticism. Damages self-esteem deeply over time. Often dismissed as "not real abuse" — but its psychological damage is severe and long-lasting.
👁️ 👁️ Digital & Stalking
Monitoring phone/social media, spreading rumours online, non-consensual sharing of images (revenge porn). Kenya's Computer Misuse Act 2018 criminalises digital GBV. Report to DCI Kenya or call 1195.

Scenario: Grace, 14, is approached by a 35-year-old family acquaintance: "You've grown up into a beautiful young woman. This stays between us — I'll give you money for your school things. Nobody needs to know."
What should Grace do?
The man says "If you tell anyone I'll say you came to me. No one will believe a girl." What does Grace do?
✅
Reported — The Bravest and Best Choice

Grace's teacher is legally required to report this to the Children's Officer. The man's threat ("no one will believe you") is itself evidence of predatory intent. In Kenya, sexual contact with anyone under 18 is statutory rape, regardless of money or "consent." Grace protected herself and future victims. GBV helpline: 1195.

⚠️
Avoided — But the Danger Continues

Avoiding him was right. But without a report, he will approach another girl. Grace's testimony could stop a pattern of abuse. You do not have to face this alone: 1195 (GBV helpline, free, 24 hours).

He contacts Grace again: "You took my money — now you owe me something back." What should she do?
✅
Recognised the Trap and Got Help

The money was bait — this is textbook grooming. Grace did not "owe" him anything. A trusted adult (teacher, parent, social worker) can help her navigate this safely. Accepting a gift never creates a debt for sex. That is coercion, not agreement.

❌
Grooming Led to Exploitation

Grace felt she had no way out once she accepted money. This is exactly how grooming works — create a sense of obligation, then demand sex. The real outcome is often pregnancy (girls under 18 account for 30% of Kenya's teenage births), STIs, and lasting trauma. It is never too late to tell someone: 1195.

❌
Secrecy Is What Predators Depend On

The request for secrecy is the clearest warning sign. Transactional sex with minors in Kenya is a crime. Adults who target young girls with money are counting on silence. Girls aged 15-19 are the group most likely to experience this form of exploitation — and most likely to become pregnant as a result. Report: 1195 (free, 24 hours).
