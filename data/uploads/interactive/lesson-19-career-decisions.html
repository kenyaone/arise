Scenario: Sylvia, 17, is told by her boyfriend: "Having a baby won't stop your dreams — my cousin has two kids and she still works. Our love is enough to make it work. You're overthinking this."
What should Sylvia think about this?
Sylvia's boyfriend says "You're being selfish — all you care about is your career." How should she respond?
✅
Clear-Sighted About Her Future — Best Outcome

Sylvia is right on both counts. In Kenya: girls who become pregnant before 18 are 6× less likely to complete secondary school. Boys who become fathers at 17 face immediate economic pressure that derails their education too. "Love" does not change economic reality. Sylvia protected both their futures.

⚠️
Held Her Ground

Sylvia held her position even under the guilt-framing. That is strength. When someone uses "selfish" to describe your self-preservation, it is a manipulation tactic. Sylvia was right.

The counsellor shows Sylvia that teenage mothers in Kenya complete an average of 4 fewer years of education than their peers. Sylvia shares this with her boyfriend. He says "But we're different." What now?
✅
Used Evidence to Hold Her Ground

This is exactly what the counsellor's information was for. "We're different" is what everyone believes — and the statistics reflect the reality of thousands of people who felt exactly that way. Sylvia's plan: stay in school, choose abstinence, complete her education. That is a plan. "Love is enough" is not.

❌
Anecdote Beat Evidence

His cousin's story is real but not representative. For every teen parent who "makes it work," many more do not complete school. Sylvia's career prospects, lifetime earnings, and health outcomes are measurably worse after a teenage pregnancy. Go back to the counsellor and ask for the full data.

❌
Romantic Belief vs Economic Reality

Love is real — but it does not pay school fees, cover hospital costs, or raise a child alone when relationships end (as many do under the pressure of unplanned parenthood). In Kenya: 44% of girls who become pregnant before 18 do not return to school. 63% of teenage mothers report their partner was not present 2 years after birth. Sylvia deserves a future built on more than hope.
